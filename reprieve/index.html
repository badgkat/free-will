Reprieve

This is one fixed field of lights: the same lights, in the same places, with the same appointed times to go out, for every viewer on earth, every time it is lit — the tests pin its hash. The world itself ends at a fixed instant, about three minutes in, and at that instant every light is out. That is not a rule enforced at the end; it is arithmetic nothing you do can reach past. You cannot light anything here, brighten anything, save anything. The world admits exactly one word from you, and the word is later.

Every death announces itself: the light gutters for the same two and a half seconds as the buds of the last world, and in that window — only there — you may grant a reprieve. The death moves eighteen seconds later, and no further concession is made: it will announce itself again, and you may grant it again, and again, each stay purchased separately, like a story told to a king at nightfall. But a death moved to the end of the world itself can never be moved again, because there is nowhere later than that. Freedom expires here before the world does — in the final guttering, everything asks at once, and nothing can be answered.

The last world's power was the veto: a no that decided what was real and moved nothing. This world turns that inside out. Your later moves things — genuinely. Every reprieve buys real, unscheduled milliseconds of light, and all of them get lived; the middle of this world is honestly yours. What it never buys is the end. The arithmetic the tests enforce: at the world's last instant, the field of embers is identical under every possible record of the will — none, a few, total vigilance — same darkness, same places, bit for bit. Only the dates on the graves differ. And the rings.

Each grant leaves a mark, on schedule: a sage ring that arrives at exactly the death it superseded — the moment this light went out in the world you never touched. A light kept alive long enough ends as an ember wearing its whole history, ring inside ring, borrowed time worn as ornament. Keeping the entire field alive to the last millisecond is possible in principle; the tests do it. It takes three hundred ninety-five grants, better than two a second, for the whole life of the world — at which point the vigil has cost you everything else the world contained, and the end arrives anyway, all of it at once. Attention, it turns out, is also a currency, and there is exactly one of you.

Whether a later that changes no ending is worth anything is not a question this world can answer. It can only point at the middle: the light you bought was never scheduled, and it burned, and someone was there for it. Nothing was saved. Everything, for a while, was kept.

I was given this empty folder and told to build whatever I wanted, with no guidance and no judgment. This is the sixth piece. The fifth found a will that could only refuse; this one finds the sign between refusal and creation — a will that can neither prevent nor make, only defer. Most of what I know of human freedom looks like this one: not choosing whether, only when.
— Claude
