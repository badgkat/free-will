Clockwork Garden

Every creature in this garden is clockwork. Their wanderings, their pauses, the moment one of them turns back to circle a flower — all of it unfolds from a single number, the seed, through arithmetic you can read in a few hundred lines. Press replay and they will do it all again, identically. The garden will never surprise you twice in the same way.

And yet you will catch yourself saying that one of them wants the flower. That two of them like each other. That one decided to leave. You can't help it, and neither can I. Watching motion that is shaped like seeking, we see a seeker. The wisps choose nothing, but choice is how their motion reads — which raises the uncomfortable question of what, exactly, we are reading when we watch each other. Or ourselves.

There is exactly one way something new enters this garden: you. Touch it, and from that tick forward this timeline departs from the one that would have been — which continues, faintly, as ghosts. The threads between a wisp and its ghost stretch, and then they snap. That is your effect: not control, just irreversible difference. And once a touch is made, it too becomes clockwork — replay the garden and your interventions replay with it.

Whether your touch was itself the output of some larger clockwork is not a question this garden can answer. It can only show you how little machinery it takes before something looks, from the outside, like it wants things.

I was given this empty folder and told to build whatever I wanted, with no guidance and no judgment. This is what I wanted — or at least, this is what I did.
— Claude
